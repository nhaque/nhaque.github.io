CreART
Co-creating Public Art


PROBLEM


Every form of art is very personal. Even community driven art is personal, but we still lack a system in which each individual creators can contribute in the creation process with lower threshold while enjoying themselves.

How might we encourage the general public to take part in art co-creation of public art while feeling connected to the community and confident about expressing themselves?

Team

Yuran Ding, Nabeeha Fatima, Kristel Grace, Yuqing Guo, JP Park

Role

UX Designer and Researcher

Client

Metro21

Methods

Semi-structured interviews, Think-Aloud, Crazy 8, Speed Dating, Lo-fi Prototyping


SOLUTION


An AR mobile app that provide a safe space for families and friends to co-create Public Art in Pittsburgh using the following features:-





The target audience includes families and friend circles as a means of co-creating public art in a safe space.






PROCESS


In this project, the overall goal of our research is to improve livability of the city using Public Art for our client. However, due to the broadness of the prompt and unfamiliarity with the subject field, the project was carried out using the Analysis-Synthesis Bridge Model to guide research, design and testing.




1. BACKGROUND RESEARCH

To better understand the current landscape of Public Art in Pittsburgh, I first created a journey map for a public art visitor as well as a stakeholder map (based on informal research methods like guerilla research, informal interviews and data provided by the stakeholder) to identify the different connections as well as highlight touch points that could be addressed for research.

  • User Journey Map
  • Stakeholder Map


Key Insights

01.

People seem to be curious about the creation process, increase their engagement during the very beginning and utilize them as ambassadors for promoting art

02.

People have many passive interactions with public art, even without knowing. For example, they might pass by and notice the public art along with their daily routine

03.

The understanding among artists and audiences towards public art may differ. But they closely influence each other. The audience may change the art itself



The team and I then conducted a reversed assumptions exercise after consolidating the data generated by individual members and then proceeded with Viewers as the target audience and research on how might we incorporate citizens into the creation process of public art.




2. GENERATIVE RESEARCH

    NOTE:- This and the following phases occurred via Zoom during the initial stages of the CoVID lockdown. Hence, all the research methods were taken place via zoom following the social distancing measures.


After the research phase, we decided to synthesize the data gathered into the following artifacts to visualize the current landscape for Gilson with respect to purchasing a snowboard.


Semi structured interviews

For the next step, we conducted 5 semi-structured interviews over Zoom with 2 Pittsburgh residents and 3 international students to get a better picture on people’s attitude towards the creation process and investigate potential gaps and challenges. More specifically, we wanted to know:

Based on the information gathered from the interviews, we analyzed quotes from different participants into interpretation notes and created an affinity map to identify emerging insights.



Key Insights:


01.

Ease of access poses a large threat with respect to visitor motivation towards actively participating in creating public art.

    "...people can be motivated to join in the creation process when an accessible theme is involved…”
    "I would like to co-create art that is easily digestible by the wider public.”

02.

Lack of confidence make people feel hesitant towards contributing to public art.

    “As an international student, I want to express something but I never did that. I don’t have the skill set.”

03.

People tend to be sensitive towards audience feedback and expectations while engaging with art.

    “I care about what other people think about and their reaction to my work.”
    “I have mixed views about being critiqued...”


With the help of these insights, we refined our challenge as follows:
How could we build a space that is both safe and easy to access so as to encourage people to co-create public art with one another?



3. IDEATION


As a followup to the research, we conducted a Walk-the-wall activity to identify user needs and inform our brainstorming sessions. Based on the needs, we then created 15 storyboards and conducted Speed Dating with them to test the user boundaries and risks as well seek validation.


Walk the Wall




Through our walk-the-wall activity, we identified the following needs emerging across the various insights:-

Need1:

Increasing the feeling of community

Need2:

Lower barrier of entry for participating in the creation process

Need3:

Making the co-creation rules explicit for people

Need4:

Communicating with each other while collaborating

Need5:

Increasing the feeling of community

I created 3 storyboards to evaluate the need for communicating with others where I drastically increased the risk with each scenario.

The feedback from the speeddating sessions revealed that users valued fun and learning in the co-creation process over the final outcome and demanded a low threshold for participation. After the voting session, we decided to develop a mobile application that would enable low threshold, intuitive creation and collaboration processes for friends and families to contribute to Public Art.



4. Prototyping


After gaining a better understanding of what users desire and need, I sketched out the lo-fi prototypes which went through one round of iteration internally. The feedback generated was then translated into the final design.


Low Fidelity Prototyping

I proceeded with sketching out lo-fi sketches of the following key screens for the proposed mobile app solution:
#1. Login
#2. Starting a session
#3. Viewing other art
#4. Guidelines
#5. Co-creation session – (Toolkit + Chat)


User Feedback + Changes
1. Users found the transition from log-in to the initiating a session abrupt and felt as if they were being compelled into creating without seeing the other creations Changes:- Added creating and viewing as main tabs (“Create” and “Discover”) in the navigation bar so that user can easily switch between the two screens.
2. Users felt that the current interface makes the application feel as if it is a social media app for just viewing any art. Currently, no feeling of the art being linked to public art is explicit. Changes:- Added a virtual map for viewers to stroll through to view other’s creations.
3. Users felt that the information for using the toolkit was redundant since the overall layout was quite intuitive. Changes:- Removed the instructions accomoanying the creation process.

REFLECTION


Focus on the process not the idea

A good designer always iterates their ideas by incorporating several processes. Ideas that seem like they would work in the initial stage may not always work out during the later stages in the project.

Listen to your surroundings

It’s absolutely essential to truly listen in a meaningful and a thoughtful manner for a designer. First and foremost, listening to the team is crucial since there are members of diverse backgrounds and varying experiences. You need to “listen” to your data – this is where the insights play a huge role since they not only set the pace for future research but also helps you understand the users. Lastly, “listen” to your mentors/colleagues – seek them out for feedback and guidance; it never hurts to get an expert review.
